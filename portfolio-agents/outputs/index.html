Designing for Panic
Case Study · Failed UPI Debit · Tier I Indian Bank

Designing
for Panic.

“In the three seconds after a payment fails and the money leaves the account, a user enters a state of biological panic.”

This is the story of building a system that manages that fear through logic.

View UPI Debit Flow →
PANIC
01 / The Crisis

The Digital Banking
Graveyard.

India's digital payment landscape is built on UPI — a marvel of engineering that occasionally stutters.

When it does, the result is a “Failed Debit.”

Trust is a currency that takes years to earn and milliseconds to lose.

For a Tier I Indian Bank, these failures weren't minor glitches. They were a leading driver of customer churn.

Existing Support Experience
  • Cryptic error messages
  • 45-minute wait times for a human agent

Both amplified the same fear:

Has my money disappeared?
The User Sees
Balance
Reduced.
1  signal
The System Sees
  • The debit may never have posted
  • The credit leg failed downstream
  • An auto-reversal is already in motion
  • Regulatory windows are still active
4+  possible states
Architectural Decision

To bridge this perception gap, we surfaced real-time ledger truth during the moment of stress.

Trade-off
Deep ledger access and higher computational overhead.
Outcome
Prevented far costlier downstream confusion and incorrect investigations.
02 / Investigation

Logic Fails
Where Fear Begins.

Research into high-anxiety users revealed: when people believe money is lost, cognition narrows.

↻ Frantic scanning of transaction history
≠ Confusion between order IDs and UTRs
! Mistyped 12-digit reference numbers

Why Traditional Chatbots Fail

Most bots rely on recall:

User explains → System classifies → Escalate

But panic collapses recall. Language becomes unreliable.

“Money got cut” could mean:
  • →  Failed UPI debit
  • →  Recurring NACH mandate
  • →  Subscription renewal
  • →  Pending refund
Language Noise Variable
vs
The Ledger Source of Truth
The Behavioral Shift

We removed recall from the equation. Instead of asking for a UTR:

“Which one of these is bothering you?”

We pulled recent transactions into a carousel.

Before Recall
→
After Recognition

The system carries the complexity.

Yes — performance demands increased. But every support request now maps to a verifiable event.

03 / The Pivot

The Machine
of Reassurance.

We stopped building a support bot.

We built a diagnostic engine.

Inquiry “What happened?”
→
Validation “Here’s what we see.”

Escalation is no longer triggered by emotion. It is triggered by verified system state.

This felt stricter at first. But strictness at entry prevents chaos downstream.

Faster resolution doesn’t come from speed.
It comes from clarity.
04 / The Engine · Failed UPI Debit · Tier I Indian Bank

The Logic
Funnel.

5-gate diagnostic engine — from complaint intake to verified escalation. Every gate prevents a specific failure mode.
Complaint Intake
01
Cooling Period
Regulatory Window Check

Transaction timestamp is verified against active regulatory windows. If within a live reversal period, escalation is temporarily unavailable.

Prevents: Duplicate Investigations
System Constraint Intentionally restricts the impulse to act instantly. Many failed debits auto-reverse during this window.
02
Debit Verification
Real-Time Ledger Poll

The ledger is queried in real time. If no debit posted, the dispute path ends immediately.

Prevents: Ghost Transaction Tickets
System Constraint Requires reliable ledger uptime. The system cannot afford ambiguity here.
03
Payment Classification
One-Time vs. Recurring Mandate

UPI one-time debits are separated from NACH recurring mandates. Distinction matters legally, operationally, and across SLA windows.

Prevents: Misrouting & SLA Violations
System Constraint Categorization adds logic complexity — but protects downstream accuracy across rail types.
04
Regulatory Alignment
Rail-Specific Escalation Rules

Escalation adapts to rail type: UPI, Card, or Mandate. Each carries distinct timelines and dispute rules reflected in real time.

Prevents: Compliance Exposure
System Constraint No refund promises. No premature liability. Interface mirrors regulatory constraints, not user impatience.
05
Active Confirmation
Truth Before Tickets

Merchant, amount, and date are restated before submission. If session is idle, state is re-validated against current ledger to prevent stale consent.

Prevents: Stale Consent Violations
System Constraint Truth before tickets.

07 / Time Design

Designing
for Time.

Support on WhatsApp is asynchronous. A dispute started at 10:00 may be resumed at 12:00. In that time, an auto-reversal may succeed.

Without re-validation: the system could escalate a solved problem.

10:00
Dispute started
10:15
Ledger silently rechecked
12:00
User returns — state re-validated
The Rule

Every 15 minutes, the ledger is silently rechecked.

If state has changed, the flow closes. This may force restarts — but it protects operational integrity.

The Operational Pivot
Before After
Escalation Trigger Complaint Driven Verified State
Classification Language Based Ledger Validated
Support Goal Speed (SLA) Correctness
Final Reflection
In high-stakes finance, reassurance doesn’t come from faster responses. It comes from making the system’s state visible — and refusing to act prematurely.

Architecture protects trust.

Safe. Logic.
Smartcoin
Case Study · Smartcoin · Fintech · 1.1 Crore Users

A Bridge
Between
Borrowing
& Saving.

“If I had money to save, why would I take a loan from you?”

This is the story of making saving feel possible for people who came only to borrow.

SAVE
01 / The Paradox

1.1 Crore Users.
Zero Savers.

SmartCoin had 1.1 crore users. Instant loans. Minimal paperwork. A product built for the underserved.

Savings features already existed — gold deposits, reward coins, micro-investments. But nobody was using them.

Many users didn’t even know savings was an option.

Every journey collapsed into:
borrow → repay → exit.
The User’s Mental Model
  • “This is a loan app. Saving is for banks.”
  • “I don’t have enough left over to save.”
  • “Where is the savings option even?”
Saving felt psychologically impossible.
02 / The Research

Read.
Analyze.
Listen.

01
Read everything — History of money, lending systems, behavioral economics of saving.
02
Analyzed 1 lakh+ users — Demographics, credit scores, repayment behavior, feature usage patterns.
03
30 interviews per segment — Motivations, financial habits, fears, and what “security” means to them.
03 / Three Personas

Not All Borrowers
Are the Same.

The Eternal Borrower
Living on
the Edge
Borrows to survive the month. Saving feels like an insult.
  • Borrow → repay → borrow again
  • Surplus is immediately spent
  • Saving = luxury, not option
Design challengeMake saving invisible
The Goal-Oriented Borrower
Disciplined
by Necessity
Borrows for milestones. Already thinks about saving.
  • Loan = investment in a goal
  • Repayment is structured
  • Needs a visible savings path
Design challengeMake saving rewarding
The Profit-Driven Investor
Credit as
Leverage
Small business owner. Loan = tool, not burden.
  • Uses credit to generate revenue
  • Tracks ROI on every rupee
  • Needs savings to compound returns
Design challengeMake saving strategic
04 / The Core Insight

Borrowing
Overshadows
Saving.

Every surface in the app pointed to one action. Every notification reinforced one identity. Every UX pattern said the same thing:

What the App Said You are a borrower.
→
What We Needed It to Say You are building stability.
The problem wasn’t that users couldn’t save. It was that the product never gave them permission to.
Three Balances to Hold
Immediate
Need vs.
Long-Term
Goal.
Design constraints
The Design Principles
  • Connect today’s needs with tomorrow’s security
  • The path to financial wellness must be visible, obvious, natural
  • Financial concepts must be demystified — not explained
Speed vs. Understanding · Borrowing vs. Saving
05 / The Solutions

Design the
Bridge.

Three interventions that moved saving from invisible to inevitable.

01
Chillar — Auto-Save

Auto-save “loose change” with each loan repayment. A small amount is rounded up and tucked away.

Saving became a side-effect of repayment — invisible, effortless, inevitable.

The Insight: Make saving a side-effect, not a decision
02
Savings Page Rebuilt

The old model: a score out of 10. Cold. Confusing. Ignored.

The new model: Save daily for 7 days = earn rewards. Visible streaks. Tangible outcomes. Emotional reinforcement.

The Insight: Turn abstract saving into a daily ritual
03
New Homepage Architecture

The old homepage: loan balance, repayment due date. That’s it. The entire product identity in two numbers.

The new homepage: Credit Score + Reward Coins + Gold Savings — surfaced before the loan balance. The visual hierarchy changed the identity of the product.

Borrow. Save. Grow.
The Insight: Architecture is identity. What you show first is what you are.

Design’s job is to build bridges. From immediate need to long-term stability. From borrowing to saving. From survival to security.

Architecture communicates identity. What you surface first is what you say you are.

Dottie — IndiGo Voice Assistant
Case Study · Dottie · IndiGo · Speculative Design

What If You

Call and

“Book me a flight from Delhi to Goa tomorrow morning.” No menus. No typing. No waiting.

Designing IndiGo’s voice assistant. A speculative exercise in translating brand identity into conversation.

DING
01 / The Brand Problem

IndiGo is a
Visual Brand.

India’s leading low-cost carrier. On-time. Professional. Reliable.

Brand voice: approachable, fun, modern. Wordplay. Slang. Emojis. “6E = Sexy.”

That voice lives entirely in visual touchpoints — ads, app copy, email.

Translating visual brand identity into a voice personality is not design. It’s linguistics.
The Design Challenge
  • How does playfulness sound in voice?
  • How do you be efficient AND warm?
  • Where does fun end and trust begin?
Brand is behavior, not just aesthetics.
02 / Two Personas, One Channel

Same Voice.
Different Needs.

The Frequent Flyer
Monthly
Work Travel
Needs efficiency. Zero friction. They know what they want.
  • Skip the pleasantries
  • Remember preferences
  • Confirm and go
GoalSpeed
The Savvy Saver
Occasional
Leisure Travel
Wants cheapest fares. Needs to understand trade-offs.
  • Show me the options
  • Explain the difference
  • Help me decide
GoalValue
03 / The Scope

What Dottie
Can & Cannot Do.

Can ✓

  • Book flights
  • Manage bookings (reschedule, cancel, check-in)
  • Add baggage, select seats
  • Answer FAQs
  • Escalate to human agent

Cannot ✗

  • Compare fares with other airlines
  • Handle unusual or edge-case requests
  • Replace human empathy in distress scenarios
  • Process payment on-call
Payment Strategy

Payment never happens on-call. A secure link is sent via SMS/WhatsApp. Dottie waits, then resumes after confirmation.

Why? Voice + payment = maximum anxiety. Separating them protects trust at the highest-stakes moment.

04 / Dottie’s Personality

Modern.
Approachable.
Fun.

“

“
Early morning flights? You must be a morning person.
“

05 / The Logic

Always
Three Options.

Dottie never shows one flight. Never shows ten. Always three — each with a clear trade-off.

01
Cheapest

Lowest fare. May have layovers, early/late times, or less legroom. For the Savvy Saver.

Trade-off: Time for money
02
Fastest

Shortest duration, most direct. May cost more. For the Frequent Flyer.

Trade-off: Money for time
03
Best Balance

Good timing + reasonable fare. The Goldilocks option.

Why three? Avoids decision paralysis. Each has a clear winner condition. Users self-select their own priority.

Cheap. Fast. Good.
The insight: No single right answer — only the right answer for you
Final Reflection
Brand identity is not visual. It’s behavioral. Dottie is IndiGo’s personality in conversation form — efficient, warm, and always three options ahead of you.

This is speculative. The thinking is real.

Beyond Touchscreens
Case Study · ICICI Bank Voicebot · Production · 70+ Live Flows

Designing
Trust
in Voice.

One misheard digit in a payment amount. One wrong card selected. Trust is gone.

India’s first transactional voicebot. No visual confirmation. No undo button. Just logic.

VOICE
01 / The Context

Not an FAQ Bot.
Transactional.

ICICI Bank. NLP-powered voicebot on phone. Not a FAQ assistant — a transactional system handling real banking actions.

Payments. Card blocks. Security resets. Dispute initiation.

High-stakes flows where one misstep is financial and regulatory liability.

Call 1800 1080. You’ll hear it live.
Scale
  • 70+ distinct live use cases
  • Millions of calls processed
  • One of India’s first of its kind
Mission-critical infrastructure.
02 / The Problem

Voice Removes
the Safety Net.

Visual interfaces give users a confirmation screen. A review step. A way to see what they’re about to do.

Voice has none of that. Everything is auditory. Intent must be inferred from spoken words under time pressure, in noisy environments, across accent diversity.

! Users interrupt mid-sentence — changing intent or adding context
! ASR errors are invisible to the user — they don’t know they were misheard
! High-anxiety context — banking + phone = maximum stress
The system must know when intent is clear enough to act — and when ambiguity requires stopping.
03 / The Architecture

Intent Is Not
Utterance.

“Block card” / “stop card” / “card is gone” / “cancel my card” — four different utterances. One intent. Different ASR confidence levels.

I built intent disambiguation hierarchies that separate the signal from the noise. Not what was said — what was meant.

What was said Utterance
→
What matters Intent
04 / The System

When to Act.
When to Stop.

Four decision layers that protect every high-stakes action.

01
Intent Disambiguation

Classify utterance into confirmed intent category. Multiple utterance patterns map to single intent. ASR confidence threshold determines certainty.

Prevents: Acting on misclassified intent
02
Mandatory Confirmation Loops

Every irreversible action requires explicit re-confirmation. Amount, card number, account — restated in voice before execution.

Prevents: Action on misheard data
03
Escalation Policy

Defined conditions under which the bot must stop and transfer to human. Not every escalation request is valid — but every ambiguous high-risk action is.

Prevents: Bot acting beyond its confidence
04
Fallback Strategy

Gentle re-prompts for low-confidence utterances. Agent transfer triggers for sustained confusion or high-value failure. Never silently fail.

Prevents: Silent failure and trust collapse
The Collaboration

This wasn’t solo design work. It was behavior specification in a cross-functional environment.

→ ML Engineers — intent training, ASR error patterns, confidence thresholds
→ Compliance Teams — mandatory confirmation language, audit trail requirements
→ Solution Architects — API orchestration, latency constraints, system boundaries
My artifact was decision logic, not mockups.
Final Reflection
In voice, there is no undo. Every decision about when to act and when to stop is baked into the architecture — before a single call is made.

The containment metrics are owned by ICICI. The conversation architecture is mine.

Trust in Voice.
Vihar — Van Vihar National Park
Case Study · Van Vihar National Park · Service Design

The
Invisible
Guide.

80% of first-time visitors never found the butterfly garden. 2,500 butterflies. 50+ species. Zero meters from the main path.

A story about doubt, discovery, and what happens when service design gets out of the way.

WILD
01 / The Problem

The Garden Was
Always There.

Van Vihar National Park, Bhopal. A butterfly garden housing 2,500 butterflies across 50+ species — one of the most remarkable experiences in the park.

80% of first-time visitors left without seeing it.

Not because it was hidden. Because the visitor journey failed to reveal it.

Discovery collapsed into checklist:
lions → tigers → crocodiles → exit.
Why the Journey Failed
  • Website: tariffs, timings, rules — no inspiration
  • Entry: cash-only queues, offline verification
  • Pathways: single road, no discovery prompts
Bureaucracy kills curiosity.
02 / Two Personas

Same Park.
Different Minds.

The Responsible Caretaker
Safety &
Clarity First
Needs predictability. Stays on the main road. Stress when uncertain.
  • Avoids unclear paths
  • Reads every sign
  • Won’t explore without permission
Design needClarity that enables
The Experience Seeker
Discovery &
Hidden Gems
Wants novelty. Explores if curious. Abandons if bureaucratic.
  • Wants the unexpected
  • Skips bureaucratic moments
  • Needs a nudge, not a guide
Design needCuriosity that rewards
03 / The Design Principles

Guide.
Support.
Subtle.

01 Guide, Don’t Dictate — The experience should feel discovered, not prescribed.
02 Support, Don’t Overshadow — Service design enables nature. It never competes with it.
03 Subtle, Not Silent — Guidance that is felt more than seen.
04 / The Invisible Guide

Five Interventions.
One Experience.

01
Giant QR at Entry

Scan for instant tickets, digital payments, park access. No queues. No cash. No friction.

Replaces: Bureaucratic entry experience
02
Living Map

Full park view. Zoom for trails, animals, utilities. Enclosure stories on tap. The butterfly garden is visible from the first screen.

Replaces: Static signage that hides options
03
Zebra Stripes

Physical path markings on ground — mirrored exactly on the digital map. The physical and digital speak the same language.

Replaces: Confusing path choices
04
Visual Stories

Short animal stories at enclosures — “The tiger rescued from captivity” — instead of dense text panels. Stories, not facts.

Replaces: Dense information boards
05
Suggested Itineraries

Curated paths matched to visitor type. “Wildlife Explorer” vs. “Family Afternoon.” The butterfly garden appears in both.

The best experiences shouldn’t require knowing to look for them.

See More. Always.
Replaces: Default single-road experience
05 / Testing

Campus Pilot.
Real Results.

Concept tested on a university campus — hidden courtyards as “attractions,” QR codes replacing docking stations.

80%
Scanned QR instantly on entering
100%
Discovered places never visited before
Treasure Hunt
How participants described the experience
The Insight

The butterfly garden wasn’t a hidden secret. It was a design failure.

Once the journey was redesigned to reveal it, people found it naturally.

Final Reflection
Great service design doesn’t add complexity. It aligns behavior with intent by removing friction. The butterfly garden was always there. We just needed to show the way.

The best guide is the one you don’t notice.

Guide. Discover.
Bhopal By Bike
Case Study · Chartered Cycles · Urban Design · Bhopal

The Curious
Case of
Bhopal’s
Unridden Cycles.

500 bikes. 100 docking stations. 2.3 million citizens. Less than 3% adoption. Five years later.

The bikes were everywhere. Nobody was riding them. This is the story of why — and what to do about it.

RIDE
01 / The Paradox

Everywhere.
But Unused.

2017. Bhopal launched Chartered Cycles — a city-wide bike-sharing network with GPS bikes and 100+ docking stations across the city.

Five years later: less than 3% of 2.3 million citizens had ever used them.

The bikes sat still. Visible everywhere. Used by almost no one.

A product visible to all, used by none — is worse than invisible.
The Visible Signs of Failure
  • Bikes at docks, never moving
  • Signage covered with advertisements
  • No one to explain how to use them
Presence is not the same as usage.
02 / The Research

78 Surveys.
11 Dock Safaris.
No Assumptions.

78 User surveys across demographics and neighborhoods
11 “Dock safaris” — observing real docking station behavior in person
→ Stakeholder interviews + app audits + comparative analysis: Yulu, Mobike, Amsterdam
03 / The Disconnect

Authority Says.
User Sees.

The most striking finding: the gap between what operators believed and what users experienced.

Topic Authorities Said Users Said
Maintenance “Twice daily” “Tyres punctured, lock problems”
Usability “Users figure it out” “Don’t know how to use them”
Features “Can’t give all comforts” “Extremely difficult to manage phone”
04 / Root Causes

Three Chains
of Failure.

01
Business Model → Neglected UX

Revenue came from advertising on docking stations — not from rides. So rides were never optimized. The customer experience was a cost center, not a revenue driver.

Result: UX investment was structurally disincentivized
02
Authority Attitude → Semantic Weakness

Instructions buried under advertisements. Signage cluttered. No one responsible for communicating how the service worked. Operators assumed users would self-educate.

Result: The service couldn’t explain itself
03
Declining Engagement → Negative Perception

Bikes everywhere but rarely used = seemed like a failed service. Users assumed if it were good, others would be using it. Low usage validated low usage.

Presence without participation reads as abandonment.

Visible. But Silent.
Result: Social proof worked against adoption
05 / The Solution: Phygital

Add a
Human Touch.

Technology alone couldn’t solve this. The barrier wasn’t digital — it was human. So the solution had to be too.

🛒
Shop at the Dock — Amul kiosk beside each dock. The shopkeeper becomes a friendly human guide who explains, demonstrates, vouches.
📋
Redesigned Posters — Clear instructions. Relatable language. Showcase affordability: “₹20 for an hour.” Show bikes as desirable, not institutional.
📱
Pop-Up Info Board + QR — Large QR code linking to a one-minute explainer video. Lower barrier than reading instructions.
🚲
Posters on Bikes — “₹20 for an hour” on the bike frame itself. Moving billboards that communicate value while cycling.
06 / Testing

60% Scan Rate.

Was Understanding.

The Proof

The barrier was not apathy. It was not price. It was not technology.

It was a fundamental failure in communication. Once people understood, they used it.

Final Reflection
The biggest problems aren’t about technology or price. They’re about a fundamental failure in communication. The bikes were ready. The city wasn’t told.

Service design is conversation at scale.

Communicate. Ride.
