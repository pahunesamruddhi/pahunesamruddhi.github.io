BHOPAL BY BIKE

THE CURIOUS CASE OF BHOPAL'S UNRIDDEN CYCLES

WANT A QUICK OVERVIEW BEFORE YOU DIVE IN?

WHERE WE START

HIDDEN IN PLAIN SIGHT

In 2017, Bhopal unveiled its Smart City icon: Chartered Cycles.
500 gleaming, GPS-enabled bikes.
100+ docking stations across the city.
A dedicated app, maintenance team, and a vision for clean, last-mile mobility.

And yet, five years later, the story was strange: the bikes sat still.
People saw them daily but almost never saw them ridden.
<3% of Bhopals 2.3M citizens had ever used them.

Our challenge was to understand:
Why does a city invest in infrastructure, only for it to become invisible?
And: How might we bring the ghost bikes back to life?

HOW THE SERVICE WORKS

THE PROBLEM

DESIGN BY ADVERTISING

Chartered Cycles had a significant physical presence, but its user experience had been neglected.


  • Misaligned model: Revenue came mainly from ads on docking stations, leaving the rental experience neglected.

  • Broken communication: Signage was cluttered with ads, burying basic instructions.

  • Perception of abandonment: Bikes were visible everywhere but rarely used, making the system seem failed or inactive.

The guiding question became: How might we increase the active user base by making the service understandable, approachable, and trustworthy?

EXPERIENCE AS AN AFTERTHOUGHT

DESIGN BY ASSUMPTION

Authorities expected users to figure things out themselves, resulting in confusing signage and a service that couldnt explain its own purpose.

THE APPROACH

RESEARCH METHOD

  • Surveys (78 respondents) to quantify awareness, usage, and barriers.

  • Dock self-safaris (11 sites) immersive observation at transit hubs, cycle lanes, tourist spots, and residential areas.

  • Stakeholder interviews with users, non-users, Chartered authorities, Smart City officials, and maintenance staff.

  • App & social media audits analyzing reviews, YouTube comments, and FAQs.

  • Comparative analysis studying Yulu, Mobike, Amsterdam cycling culture.

  • Concept testing using a suggestion box pilot on campus as a low-risk analog.

SELF SAFARI

Instead of observing from afar, we experienced the confusion and friction firsthand.

THE DISCONNECT

THE TALE OF TWO SERVICES

The authorities and the users werent talking about the same service. One spoke in promises, the other in frustrations. Side by side, the gap was impossible to ignore.

MAINTENANCE

Authorities: Maintenance is carried twice a day.

Users: Tyres were punctured most times Lock ki bohot problems hoti hai.

USABILITY

Authorities: We cant tell everything to our users, few things they will have to figure out on their own.

Users: Ive seen them on the road, but I dont know how to use them.

FEATURES

Authorities: We cant give all the comforts to the users what if somebody steals the phone stand?

Users: Its extremely difficult to manage the phone while carrying my cycle.

THE INSIGHT

ROOT CAUSE ANALYSIS

Chain 1: Business Model Neglected UX

Cause: Revenue came mainly from ads, not rides. We dont want to earn any income from the cycles."

Effects: Poor maintenance, missing features like phone holders, and general neglect.

Chain 2: Authorities Attitude Semantic Weakness

Cause: Users were expected to figure it out.

Effects: Confusing signage, hidden app features, widespread user confusion, and over-reliance on customer care.

Chain 3: Declining Engagement Negative Perception

Cause: Initial promotion faded, curiosity declined.

Effects: Few visible riders, cycles seen as rotting, reinforcing the sense of failure.

THE SYNTHESIS

EMPTY BIKES ARE JUST THE TIP

What the public saw were empty bikes the visible tip of the iceberg. But beneath the surface lay deeper, hidden causes: low rider value, unclear instructions, and neglected maintenance.

THE BEHAVIORAL STATEMENT

"When potential first-time users, curious about the service, want to take a convenient ride, they will abandon the attempt after encountering confusing, ad-cluttered, and unreadable instructions at the docking station, as measured by non-user interviews, observational studies at the docks, and the service's extremely low user acquisition rate."

THE SYNTHESIS

THE DESIGN PRINCIPLES

The core issues werent isolated flaws but tensions built into the service. From these tensions, we derived design principles to guide our solutions:

Automation vs. Assurance Add a Human Touch

Efficiency came at the cost of trust. Design must reintroduce human guidance to support riders when things go wrong.

Visibility vs. Understanding Communicate, not Exist

Bikes were visible everywhere but still misunderstood. Design must turn presence into clear, simple communication.

Commerce vs. Service Service that serves both

Ad revenue took priority over rentals. A better ride experience must be the priority, while still supporting the revenue goals of the system.

IDEATION

Our design strategy focused on a concept we called the phygital solution treating the physical docks and the digital app as one seamless, human-centered experience.

THE ROADS NOT TAKEN

THE FILTERING

Our design strategy focused on a concept we called the phygital solution - stop viewing the physical docking stations and the digital app as separate entities. Instead, the aim was to merge them into a single, seamless, and intuitive experience for the user, putting the human element back at the centre of the service

With dozens of raw ideas, we needed focus. We applied three filters:


  1. Impact Does it improve understanding, draw users to the dock, or spark curiosity?

  2. Feasibility Is it low-cost, easy to implement, and sustainable?

  3. Ease of Testing Can it be validated quickly on a small scale?


This narrowed the list to a promising set: adding kiosks, simplifying info with QR codes, using local slang, and highlighting pricing + fitness.

ready for a new ride?

AT THE DOCK

The journey begins at a revamped docking station. Instead of a silent, confusing panel, there's life.

SHOP AT THE DOCK

A small food kiosk from a popular brand like Amul now sits beside the dock. It naturally attracts people and, more importantly, the shopkeeper acts as a friendly human guide, ready to answer questions and build trust

COMMUNICATE TO BE UNDERSTOOD

The old, text-heavy posters are gone. When there are no advertisements, in their place are redesigned posters with clear instructions to feel relatable and showcase the service's affordability. A quirky pop-up info board catches your eye. It features a large, obvious QR code, inviting you to scan

THE FIRST SCAN

You scan the QR code. It doesn't take you to a complicated app store page. Instead, it opens a simple web extension with a one-minute video that clearly explains how the service works. All the initial confusion is gone. Now armed with understanding, you're ready to download the app.

ON THE CYCLE

As you ride, the cycle itself continues the communication. The info strip on the back has been redesigned to be more graphical and less text-heavy. Simple posters on the cycle's leaf highlight the key selling points, like "20 for an hour," turning every bike into a moving billboard that spreads the message across the city

DESIGN IN ACTION

WHAT CHANGED

By the end of this new journey, the guide is almost invisible. The kiosk, the slang, the QR code, and the video work together to restore the three balances:


The kiosk adds a human touch to an otherwise cold, automated system.

The clear, relatable messaging ensures the service can communicate its value, not just exist as infrastructure.

The entire flow is redesigned to put the rider first, focusing on clarity and ease of use.


But would this human-centred approach actually work in the real world?

CONCEPT TESTING

MAKING THE INVISIBLE WORK

To test our theory, we found a perfect parallel on our college campus: the director's suggestion box. Like the bikes, everyone saw it, but almost no one used it because they didn't understand how it worked or if it was effective.

We placed the box next to the campus Amul kiosk (high footfall) and created posters with student slang and a QR code linked to a simple explanatory webpage and video

The results were remarkable:

45 people who came to the kiosk walked up to the box.

27 people (60%) were curious enough to scan the code to understand the service.

13 people (a 48% conversion rate) went on to actually use the suggestion box.


The experiment proved that the barrier wasn't apathy; it was a lack of understanding. By focusing on clear communication, a local voice, and adding a simple human touch, we proved we could bring a dormant service back to life. The case of the ghost bikes was officially cracked. The designed changes were later shown to Chartered Cycles authorities, who gave positive feedback.

REFLECTION

LOOKING BACK

This project was a fascinating challenge that reinforced a core principle: often, the biggest problems aren't about technology or price, but about a fundamental failure in communication.


Our role as researchers was to decode that failure and translate it into a human-centred solution.The mystery of Bhopal's ghost bikes wasn't a technical one. It was an information problem. The service wasn't broken; it was just misunderstood.
