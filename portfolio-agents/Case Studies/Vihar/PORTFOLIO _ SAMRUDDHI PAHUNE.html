A STORY ABOUT DOUBT, DISCOVERY AND AN INVISIBLE GUIDE

WANT A QUICK OVERVIEW BEFORE YOU DIVE IN?

WHERE WE START

THE HIDDEN GARDEN, UNSEEN

80% of first-time visitors to Van Vihar missed the butterfly garden home to 2,500 butterflies of 50+ species. The reason wasnt disinterest. It was that the visitor journey simply failed to reveal it.


Our challenge was to understand why discovery kept failing and how we might reimagine the journey so that wonder could surface naturally, without friction, stress, or chance.

CREDITS - film by Tushar Bhojwani on Van Vihar National Park and Zoo, Bhopal

THE PROBLEM

DESIGN BY NEGLECT

Over the years, Van Vihar had grown into a sprawling park, but its visitor experience hadnt kept pace.

  • The website was transactional: tariffs, timings, rules no inspiration.

  • The entry felt bureaucratic: cash-only queues, even online bookings required offline verification.

  • The pathways pushed people down a single road, with little signal as to what lay beyond the forks.

In practice, this meant discovery collapsed into a checklist: lions, tigers, crocodiles then exit. Everything else, including the butterfly garden, was invisible.

The guiding question became:

How might we increase the proportion of first-time visitors who discover hidden attractions, without adding time, stress, or effort?

THE APPROACH

BALANCING GUIDANCE AND WONDER

Revealing wonder for everyone, without losing the magic

Three primary questions informed our design strategy:

  1. How do you reduce doubt without over-directing exploration?

  2. What contexts limit discovery for different visitors (caretakers vs. seekers)?

  3. What does the perfect visit look like emotionally, spatially, and temporally?

Early on, it was clear that designing for Van Vihar wasnt about adding signs or features. It was about balancing two opposing forces: the Responsible Caretakers need for clarity, and the Experience Seekers desire for curiosity.

IMMERSION

We traced the rhythms of the park not as designers, but as wanderers, trying to understand how a place like Van Vihar reveals itself to a stranger.

SHADOWING

We created a blueprint mapping the entire visitor journey: from website and ticketing, to paths and attractions, to exit. This helped align frontstage (visitor experience) with backstage (systems, staff, processes). The blueprint revealed a central tension: the system was optimized for transactions (timings, tickets, rules), not experiences (discovery, stories, delight).

THE PEOPLE

WHO ARE WE DESIGNING FOR

Two Archetypes, Two Needs

We synthesized the research into two design anchors:


The Responsible Caretaker

Motivated by: safety, clarity, predictability.

Behaviours: stay on the main road, avoid unclear paths, stick to visible attractions.

Pain: stress when things are uncertain.


The Experience Seeker

Motivated by: novelty, hidden gems, stories.

Behaviours: explore if curiosity is sparked, but skip unclear or bureaucratic moments.

Pain: magic flattened into transactions.

Design Principle: Reduce stress for caretakers, amplify wonder for seekers.

SERVICE BLUEPRINT

Blueprinting the journey revealed the core problem: The system optimized for transactions, not experiences.

"When first-time visitors with limited knowledge of the parks layout want to see the highlights and make the most of their trip, they will stay on the main road and only visit obvious attractions (lions, tigers, crocodiles) as measured by entry/exit tracking, observational shadowing, and self-reported awareness of missed attractions"

THE INSIGHT

THE THREE BALANCES

As we synthesized research, it became clear that the challenge wasnt just visitors miss attractions. It was deeper: their experience sat on a tension between -

CLARITY X CURIOSITY

Too much clarity flattens the visit into a checklist.
Too much curiosity without anchors leaves people uncertain.
The balance lies in guiding just enough to let wonder unfold.

NATURE X SERVICE

Nature invites freedom and immersion.
Service layers often impose bureaucracy and control.
The balance lies in systems that support without overshadowing.

VISIBLE X INVISIBLE

Guidance that is too visible kills curiosity.
Guidance that is too invisible leaves discovery to chance.
The balance lies in subtle cues felt more than seen.

THE SYNTHESIS

THE DESIGN PRINCIPLES

From the three balances, we defined guiding principles for ideation. These became the lens through which every intervention was evaluated.

Clarity Curiosity Guide, Dont Dictate

Design should reduce doubt without flattening discovery.

Visitors need just enough orientation to feel safe, and just enough openness to feel wonder.

Nature Service Support, Dont Overshadow

Services should enable nature, not compete with it.

Ticketing, navigation, and systems must fade into the background so the park remains the hero.

Visible Invisible Subtle, Not Silent

Guidance should be felt more than seen.

Cues must be present enough to orient, quiet enough to preserve curiosity.

IDEATION

Using these principles as anchors, we sketched interventions across touchpoints from the entry experience to wayfinding to storytelling. We explored ideas that could act as an Invisible Guide: subtle, systemic cues that restore balance without breaking immersion

THE ROADS NOT TAKEN

WHAT WE LEFT BEHIND

We filtered ideas by testing them against our three design principles. Some didnt make the cut:


Park-wide Signage Broke Guide, Dont Dictate
too much clarity, no curiosity.

Painted Arrows Broke Subtle, Not Silent
guidance became heavy-handed.

Treasure Hunt Broke Support, Dont Overshadow
game overshadowed nature.

Audio Guides Broke Support, Dont Overshadow
tech distracted from the environment.


Why it matters: Saying no was as important as saying yes. It kept the Invisible Guide true to balance gentle, systemic, and in harmony with the park.

AT THE GATE

We begin at the entrance. No long queues, no fumbling for cash.
Instead, a bold but quiet prompt a giant QR. We pause, curious, and scan.

In seconds, everything opens up: tickets, payments, and the park itself. The day starts not with rules, but with a question: What might we find in here?

ON THE ROAD

We head down the main path. But this time, the road doesnt trap us.
On our phone, a living map unfurls. At a glance, the entire park. Zoom in, and trails, animals, and utilities appear. Zoom further, and enclosures breathe with icons and stories.

You look down zebra stripes painted on the path. You look up the same stripes mirrored on the map. Suddenly, a fork isnt confusing. Its an invitation. You don't want to look at your phone, no worries, let the path guide you!

AT THE ENCLOSURE

We stop at the tiger enclosure. No dense text panels, no dry signage.
Instead, a tap on the map reveals a short, visual story: a tiger rescued from captivity, now thriving here.
At the bear enclosure, another story unfolds survival, care, recovery.
The park stops being a checklist of species. It becomes a place of encounters.

DESIGN IN ACTION

WHAT CHANGED

By the end of the walk, we hardly notice the guide. The map, the stripes, the QR, the itineraries, the stories

none shouted, none dictated.


But together, they restored balance:

clarity without control,

curiosity without confusion,

service without overshadowing nature.


And yet, this was only our walk-through.

The real question remained:
Would first-time visitors feel it too? Would they discover the butterfly garden, not by accident, but by design?

That question led us to our next step concept testing.

CONCEPT TESTING

FROM PARK TO CAMPUS

Before bringing the Invisible Guide back to Van Vihar, we needed a safe, controllable space to test the concept. We piloted it on a university campus reimagining hidden courtyards, gardens, and nooks as attractions. The goal was simple: would subtle cues actually change behaviour?

We piloted on a university campus, reimagining hidden spots as attractions.

  • 80% scanned QR instantly hesitation gone.

  • Students discovered new places theyd never visited.

  • Described the experience as a treasure hunt.

Proof that removing friction unlocks curiosity and curiosity unlocks wonder.

REFLECTION

LOOKING BACK

This project reinforced a principle I carry forward:

Great service design doesnt add complexity; it aligns behavior with intent by removing friction.

As a researcher, my role wasnt just surfacing pain points. It was turning them into behavioral goals, measurable outcomes, and frameworks that guided design ensuring solutions reduced stress for caretakers while amplifying wonder for seekers.

The future of Van Vihar isnt more technology its deeper connection. By making discovery effortless, we make space for wonder. By letting technology disappear, we let nature shine.
