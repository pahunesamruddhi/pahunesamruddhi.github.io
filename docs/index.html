Case Study · Failed UPI Debit · Tier I Indian Bank

Designing
for Panic.

“In the three seconds after a payment fails and the money leaves the account, a user enters a state of biological panic.”

This is the story of building a system that manages that fear through logic.

View UPI Debit Flow →
PANIC
01 / The Crisis

The Digital Banking
Graveyard.

India's digital payment landscape is built on UPI — a marvel of engineering that occasionally stutters.

When it does, the result is a “Failed Debit.”

Trust is a currency that takes years to earn and milliseconds to lose.

For a Tier I Indian Bank, these failures weren't minor glitches. They were a leading driver of customer churn.

Existing Support Experience
  • Cryptic error messages
  • 45-minute wait times for a human agent

Both amplified the same fear:

Has my money disappeared?
The User Sees
Balance
Reduced.
1  signal
The System Sees
  • The debit may never have posted
  • The credit leg failed downstream
  • An auto-reversal is already in motion
  • Regulatory windows are still active
4+  possible states
Architectural Decision

To bridge this perception gap, we surfaced real-time ledger truth during the moment of stress.

Trade-off
Deep ledger access and higher computational overhead.
Outcome
Prevented far costlier downstream confusion and incorrect investigations.

CS 03 · Fintech · Behavioral Design · UX Research · 2023

A Bridge Between
Borrowing
and Saving.

A belief that was structurally true under one design became structurally false under a different one. Not because we convinced anyone. Because we changed where the journey goes next.
SAVE
01 / The Setup

SmartCoin Was
Killing It.

1.1 crore users. Instant loans. Minimal paperwork.

For India’s underbanked — micro-merchants, blue-collar workers, self-employed earners falling between formal banking and microfinance — it was the financial lifeline formal institutions refused to be.

But here’s what SmartCoin already had: savings features. Gold deposits. Reward coins. Investment tools. A financial wellness platform, fully built, sitting inside a lending app.

In practice: nobody was using any of it.

And when you looked at why, every user journey told the same story.

02 / Every Journey Ended at the Same Place

Three Roads.
One Wall.

Picture the app from a user’s point of view.

You open SmartCoin. You need a loan. You apply. You get it. You return to the homepage.

You make repayments. You earn reward coins for paying on time. You return to the homepage.

You repay the loan in full. You return to the homepage.

The homepage was a wall dressed as a welcome screen. Every moment of meaningful financial behavior — getting a loan, making a repayment, earning a reward — deposited the user back at the same blank slate with no thread to pull, no continuation offered.

Three parallel journeys. None of them touching.

Borrow → Repay → Homepage.
Earn rewards → Homepage.
Discover savings (somehow) → Homepage.
03 / The Belief

One User Said What
the Data Was Showing.

“अगर मेरे पास बचाने के लिए पैसा होता तो मैं आपसे कर्ज क्यों लेता?”

“If I had money to save, why would I take a loan from you?”

Brutal. Honest. Airtight.

This wasn’t resistance. This was a user applying perfect logic to the information the product was giving them.

The design question wasn’t “how do we add a savings feature.”

It was: how do you connect the journey that already exists to the section they’ve never been shown?

04 / The Research

No Assumptions.
No Ready-Made Personas.

The approach: start with 1 lakh+ users, chip away until real people emerge.

Phase 1 — The UI Audit

Screens became evidence.

The interface didn’t just reflect the disconnect between borrowing and saving. It amplified it.

Phase 2 — Narrowing 1 Lakh Users to Real Segments

Phase 3 — Giving the Segments a Voice

Numbers say what. They never say why.

Thirty users per segment. Semi-structured interviews. Borrowing motivations, savings behavior, financial habits, fears.

The critical methodological finding:

From the outside, the Eternal Borrower and the Profit-Driven Investor looked identical — both maxed out loans every time, both repaid reliably. Without the qualitative work, they would have been collapsed into one. They weren’t the same person at all.

05 / The Three People

Three People.
Three Reasons.
Same Dead End.

The Eternal Borrower
Living on
the Edge
Rent, groceries, school fees — everything consumes more than they earn. The gap is permanent. SmartCoin is their oxygen.
  • Motivated by: Survive the month. Keep afloat.
  • Always borrows the full limit
  • Repayment is top priority — sometimes at the cost of everything else
The belief“If I had money to save…” Saving isn’t just out of reach. It’s advice from another planet.
The Goal-Oriented Borrower
Borrowing
With a Plan
Borrowing is purposeful. A medical bill. School fees. A family event. They’ve compared options. They came to SmartCoin specifically.
  • Motivated by: Achieve a goal. Plan for stability.
  • Borrows only what’s necessary
  • Already thinks about saving — but the tools don’t connect
The beliefThere’s no bridge between this loan and my actual goals.
The Profit-Driven Investor
Borrowing as
Leverage
A small business owner. A loan isn’t debt — it’s leverage. Money borrowed today is capital to grow tomorrow.
  • Motivated by: Business expansion. ROI.
  • Borrows maximum amounts. Repays early to cut interest.
  • Extra cash goes straight back into the business
The beliefEvery rupee saved is a rupee not working.

What all three had in common:

None of them were saving. Three personas. Three different reasons. The same dead end.

06 / The Three Tensions

Three Traps
Baked Into
the Product.

Three structural tensions baked into the product itself. Each one a trap the obvious solutions would fall straight into.

01
Immediate Need vs. Long-Term Goal

SmartCoin delivered instant loans flawlessly. But it never surfaced a path beyond the loan. The app solved today’s pain — and erased tomorrow’s possibility.

02
Speed vs. Understanding

The product optimized for frictionless borrowing. In that rush, financial concepts were left unexplained. Confidence in anything beyond the loan stayed low.

03
Borrowing vs. Saving

By treating these as separate, competing behaviors — physically separated in the app, never connected in the journey — the product confirmed the belief: saving is only for people with surplus.

07 / The Design Principles

Three Tensions.
Three Principles.

01
Connect, Don’t Silo

Borrowing and saving must feed each other. Every action must thread to a next step.

02
Surface, Don’t Bury

The path to financial wellness must appear at the moment it’s relevant — not three taps deep, but at the end of the journey the user just completed.

03
Clarify, Don’t Overwhelm

One screen, one job. Confidence grows only when clarity is present.

08 / The Work

Turning Dead Ends
Into Continuations.

CHILLAR — The Hero

Chillar: loose change.

The belief: saving requires surplus. Borrowers have none.

CHILLAR doesn’t argue with this. It routes around it entirely.

Every time a user makes an EMI repayment, a small amount is set aside automatically. Not a new decision. Not a new habit. Just a shadow attached to something they were already going to do.

Repayment no longer ends at the homepage. It opens a savings prompt. The loan journey and the savings journey are threaded together for the first time.

CHILLAR by Persona
What was once the end
of the journey —
repayment —
became the beginning
of a new one.
How It Works for Each Person
  • For the Eternal Borrower: The belief “I can’t save because I’m borrowing” becomes structurally false. Repayment is saving now. No mental model shift required.
  • For the Goal-Oriented Borrower: Savings goals pre-populate based on their borrowing purpose and repayment stage. The app starts connecting what they’re doing to what they’re trying to achieve.
  • For the Profit-Driven Investor: The calculator frames tiny deposits as compounding business capital. ₹10 a day. ₹3,650 a year. Float available the next time an opportunity arrives.

Rewards — Closing a Loop That Was Leaking

Users were earning reward coins after every on-time repayment. Almost none knew. The coins sat silently. The only redemption path — digital gold — was a product they neither understood nor trusted.

Two fixes:

Expand what coins can buy. Discounts on processing fees (the cost users complained about most). Free credit score checks. Coins became currency for things users already wanted.

Close the loop at the moment of earning. After any repayment, referral, or daily saving — a popup. “You’ve earned X coins. Convert them now.” The earning and the redeeming happen in the same breath.

Repayment → coins earned → prompt → savings page.

A journey that previously ended now has three more steps, each moving the user deeper into their own financial picture.

Savings Page — Rebuilt for Real People

The old savings page was designed for someone who already understood digital gold. Users called it confusing, irrelevant, scary.

The old score: abstract, out of 10, measuring something users couldn’t name.

The new model: save daily for 7 days → earn rewards. A concrete challenge. An achievable action. A container that fills visibly as you go.

Simplification — One Screen, One Job

The loan application became a conversation that knew where it was going. Each step showed what came next. Completion felt achievable.

For the Eternal Borrower: reduced cognitive load during the moment of peak financial stress.

For the Goal-Oriented Borrower: reassurance that the process ends somewhere aligned with their need.

For the Profit-Driven Investor: their time is respected.

Help — A Conversation, Not a List

The old help section: select a category, face forty questions, find your own answer. For users with urgent money questions, the cognitive load of finding help was higher than the cognitive load of not asking.

The redesign: sequential elimination. Step by step. Conversational. Calm. The same diagnostic logic used in the UPI dispute engine — because financial anxiety narrows cognition the same way payment panic does.

10 / Reflection

The Failure Wasn’t
the Loan Product.

The failure was an app that reset after every meaningful action — returning users to a blank starting point when it could have shown them where to go next.

Borrow → Repay → Homepage was never a neutral design decision. It was a choice. And it confirmed, silently, every time: this app is for borrowing. The rest is for someone else.

CHILLAR works not because it teaches users to save. It works because it attaches saving to something users were already doing — and makes it automatic. No new mental model. No surplus required. Just repayment, doing one more thing than it did before.

The reward popup works because the moment you’ve just earned something is the only moment the redemption prompt feels natural.

The connected homepage works because visibility alone isn’t discoverability. Putting savings three taps deep isn’t hiding it — but putting it at zero taps after a repayment is something entirely different.

Don’t ask people to find the savings section.
Put the savings section at the end of the journey they’re already on.
Final Reflection
The bridge was already built.
We just connected the roads.
Connected. Not Convinced.
CS 04 · Conversational Design · Voice UX · Brand Systems · Speculative

What If You
Could Call
and Book
a Flight?

A bot without personality is just a menu with a voice. Personality isn’t decoration in a conversation. It’s what carries the relationship when things go slightly wrong.
DING
01 / The Spark

Book Me a Flight
from Delhi to Goa.

“Book me a flight from Delhi to Goa tomorrow morning.”

No menus. No typing. No waiting on hold. Just a voice, a request, and a response.

That was the imagination spark behind Dottie — a speculative design exercise exploring what it would mean for IndiGo, India’s largest low-cost carrier, to have a voice assistant worthy of its brand.

This wasn’t an official IndiGo project. It was a design question asked seriously: if a bot is a brand ambassador, what does that actually require?

The answer is more interesting than it first appears.

02 / The Brand It Had to Carry

What Is IndiGo,
in Personality Terms?

IndiGo doesn’t just sell seats. It sells personality. Its advertising uses wordplay, local slang, and a tone that is simultaneously professional and irreverent.

It is the airline that says “on time, every time” and means it — and also the airline whose campaigns make you smile.

A bot representing this brand couldn’t be a transaction machine. It had to carry the voice.

Three qualities that defined the brief:

01 Approachable — friendly, informal, easy to talk to. Never corporate.
02 Fun — playful, a little cheeky, willing to crack a small joke.
03 Modern — rooted in how people actually speak today. Slang. Contractions. Human rhythm.
A bot that says “Your booking has been confirmed” is doing a job.
A bot that says “Ding! You’re all set for Goa — get packing” is doing a job and being IndiGo.
03 / The Problem It Was Solving

A Menu in
Conversational Clothing.

IndiGo’s existing booking touchpoints each had a specific failure mode.

× Website & app — rich in options but heavy to navigate. Built for completeness, not speed.
× Contact center — waiting, IVR menus designed in the nineties, agents who were helpful but couldn’t scale.
× Existing chatbot — a menu in disguise. No personality. When something went outside its path, it escalated robotically or failed.

For a brand whose promise is on time, every time — the act of booking was consistently out of sync with the brand it represented.

The gap Dottie was designed to fill: a booking experience that moves at conversation speed, carries IndiGo’s voice, and handles the unexpected with the same ease it handles the routine.

04 / The Two People

Same Voice.
Different Needs.

Two users. Two entirely different relationships with the same booking task.

The Frequent Flyer
Zero
Friction
Doesn’t need to be charmed. Needs speed. Every extra question is a failure.
  • Need: fast, smart, almost invisible
  • Every step that could have been inferred from context is a failure
  • Skip the pleasantries, confirm and go
Dottie’s jobGet out of the way.
The Savvy Saver
Transparency
Before Trust
Not suspicious — just burned by hidden charges before.
  • Need: surface options clearly, name trade-offs honestly
  • Make them feel like they found the best deal, not sold one
  • Easily lost to a competitor if the experience hides something
Dottie’s jobEarn trust through honesty.

The same bot. Two very different modes it has to be capable of.

05 / Introducing Dottie

IndiGo
in a Voice.

Not a generic assistant that happens to work for IndiGo. A character with a specific personality, a specific vocabulary, and a specific way of handling when a conversation doesn’t go as planned.

What Dottie sounds like:

“ On a booking: “Ding! You’re set for Goa. Get packing.”
“ On small talk: “Early morning flights? You must be a morning person.”
“ On a difficult moment: “I know this isn’t ideal, but let’s fix it together.”

Dottie’s personality is most load-bearing in the difficult moments, not the easy ones.

Any system can confirm a booking pleasantly. Only a character can turn a cancelled flight into a moment where the brand relationship survives.

What Dottie can do

  • Book new tickets: one-way, round trip, multi-passenger
  • Manage bookings: reschedule, cancel, check-in, add baggage
  • Answer common queries: baggage, check-in timings, meals, offers
  • Escalate gracefully to a human agent when outside her scope

What Dottie cannot do

  • Compare fares with other airlines — Dottie is IndiGo, not a search engine
  • Handle unusual requests outside the IndiGo ecosystem
  • Replace human empathy in genuinely complex cases

The limits are as designed as the capabilities. Dottie’s graceful escalation — “This one needs a human touch, let me connect you” — is not a failure state. It’s a feature.

06 / How Dottie Thinks

Always
Three Options.

Before the conversation flows, one design decision deserves its own moment: how Dottie suggests flights.

Not by listing every option. By negotiating trade-offs.

01
Cheapest

Lowest fare, may have stops. For the Savvy Saver.

02
Fastest

Shortest duration, direct where possible. For the Frequent Flyer.

03
Best Balance

Good timing, reasonable fare — Dottie’s recommendation.

“I found three flights: Cheapest at ₹6,990 (1 stop), Fastest at ₹7,669 (direct), and a Balanced option at ₹7,313 (non-stop, afternoon). Which works best for you?”

The three-option structure matches how people actually decide under moderate uncertainty — they don’t want every option, they want a curated shortlist with trade-offs already named.

Payment rule: Payments never happen on-call. Dottie sends a secure link via SMS or WhatsApp. If payment fails: transfer to human. Always.

Dottie never lets a financial transaction become a dead end.

07 / The Three Conversations

Designed from
the Edges Inward.

Not the happy path. The moments the happy path doesn’t account for.

If we can handle the error path with grace, the happy path takes care of itself.

The Happy Path

The happy path is clean, fast, and — because of Dottie’s voice — warm. Its most important feature is what it doesn’t do: it doesn’t ask for information that can be inferred. It doesn’t repeat itself. It doesn’t sound like a form being read aloud.

The Alternative Path

Real conversations don’t follow a script. People change their mind, remember something they forgot, ask a question mid-sentence.

This is where Dottie’s design as a negotiating system — not a scripted flow — becomes visible. She tracks what she knows, updates when she gets new information, and never punishes the user for not knowing exactly what they wanted when the conversation started.

For the Savvy Saver who asks “actually, is there anything cheaper on Friday?” — Dottie doesn’t start over. She pivots, surfaces the options, and remembers everything else.

The Error Path

This is the most important path. And the one most voicebots get wrong.

When Dottie doesn’t understand, she has three options — in order:

1 Clarify once. “Just to check — did you mean Delhi or Dehradun?” One attempt. Not three.
2 Rephrase and try. “It sounds like you want to reschedule — is that right?”
3 Escalate gracefully. “This one’s a bit outside my lane — let me get someone who can help.” No failure language. Clean handoff.

Dottie never loops. She never asks the same question twice. She never makes the user feel trapped.

The error path isn’t the failure state.
How a bot handles the error path is the brand.
08 / The Technical Layer

How Dottie
Understands.

Intents & Entities

Intents — what the user wants to do: book a flight, manage a booking, ask a question.

Entities — the details needed: origin, destination, date, passenger count, PNR, class, add-ons.

Dottie’s job is to collect missing entities conversationally — not all at once, but as they naturally arise.

NLU Training Design

Consistent tagging: dates, cities, numbers, Indian English (“tomorrow morning” = 7–11 AM).

ASR error handling: “Delhi” misheard as “Belly” is a real problem. Training includes phonetic confusables for high-frequency city names.

Edge cases aren’t failures — they’re training data. The feedback loop is designed into the system.

The Ranking Logic

Non-stop > fewer stops > better on-time record. Price is a filter, not a default sort. The user’s stated preference always overrides the default.

09 / What Dottie Is, Actually

Not a Tool.
A Character.

Dottie is not a utility that processes booking requests.

She is IndiGo’s voice — the personality the brand projects when no human is available to project it.

Every word she says either builds or erodes the relationship IndiGo has with the person on the other end of the call.

That’s why the error path matters as much as the happy path. That’s why the three-option flight suggestion matters. That’s why “Ding! You’re set for Goa” matters over “Your booking has been confirmed.”

In a conversation, personality isn’t how you start.
It’s how you recover.
Design the recovery first. The rest follows.

11 / Reflection

The Design Questions
That Were Real.

This was a speculative project. Dottie doesn’t exist in IndiGo’s systems today. But the design questions it forced were real:

What does it mean to translate a brand into a voice?

Not into a logo or a colour palette, but into the specific words a machine chooses when a user changes their mind mid-sentence.

Where does personality become load-bearing?

Not in the confirmation message — in the moment the user asks something the system didn’t expect.

What does responsible AI design look like in a consumer product?

Dottie has explicit limits. She doesn’t try to do everything. She hands off gracefully. She never makes the user feel trapped. These aren’t constraints on the design — they are the design.

For me, Dottie was the project where conversational design, brand systems, and AI thinking had to work together — or the whole thing collapsed.

A bot is only as trustworthy as its worst moment.
Design the worst moments first.
Final Reflection
IndiGo in a voice.
Reliable when it matters.
Fun when it can be.

CS 01 (Ongoing) · Conversational AI · Financial UX · Trust Architecture

Designing
Trust
in Voice.

Most design problems ask you to reduce friction. This one asked us to reduce friction and eliminate error simultaneously — in a medium built for neither.
VOICE
01 / The Moment

Picture Someone
Who Has Just Realised
Their Card Is Missing.

Not lost-in-the-other-bag missing. Gone. Someone has it.

They need to block it. Now. Before a transaction goes through.

They don’t have time to find the app, log in, navigate to card controls. They’re on the street, or in a meeting, or on a train platform. They pick up the phone and call the bank.

In 2022, what they got was an IVR menu. Press 1 for Hindi. Press 2 for account services. Press 3 for—

By the time they reached a human, the card had been used.

ICICI Bank set out to change this.

02 / What Was Built

A Transactional,
NLP-Powered
Voicebot.

Not a FAQ assistant. Not a menu in voice clothing.

A system capable of handling high-stakes financial actions — in natural language, on a phone call — with over 70 live use cases ranging from bill payments and balance checks to card blocking, PIN resets, and security controls.

Try it live: call 1800 1080.

One of the first voicebots in India where a customer could say “block my card” and have it actually happen — verified, confirmed, executed — without pressing a single key or speaking to a human.

My role: Lead Designer. End-to-end conversational experience. Flow architecture, microcopy, ML collaboration, compliance alignment, and the specific design logic required when every flow is mission-critical.

03 / The Challenge

Money
Is Emotional.

People don’t forgive easily when it’s their savings, their card, their identity.

This is the first thing to understand about designing financial voice experiences — and the thing that makes every design decision different from designing for any other domain.

In most UX work, a confusing moment costs the user a few seconds of frustration. Here, a confusing moment could mean:

× A transaction going through that the user meant to block.
× A card staying active that the user thought was frozen.
× A PIN reset completed on an account the user didn’t intend.
The cost of misunderstanding isn’t friction. It’s broken trust. And in banking, broken trust doesn’t recover easily.

Four Structural Hurdles

1 — Complex financial rules

Every transaction required airtight compliance. Every flow had legal, regulatory, and security constraints that couldn’t be softened for conversational ease. The design had to work within these constraints, not around them.

2 — Human unpredictability

Customers interrupt. They mumble. They change their mind mid-sentence. They get anxious and go quiet. They say “stop my card” and “block my card” and “my card’s gone” — all meaning the same thing. The system had to be designed for how people actually speak under stress, not how they speak when calm and deliberate.

3 — Massive scale

70+ distinct flows, each mission-critical. A single IVR redesign is hard. Building 70 conversation architectures — each with its own compliance requirements, fallback logic, and confirmation protocols — at the same time, is something else entirely.

4 — Technical constraints

Latency. Noisy call environments. Accent diversity across India. ASR errors that turn “NEFT transfer” into something unrecognisable. The gap between what NLP can do in ideal conditions and what it can do when someone is calling from a busy street in a second language.

This wasn’t about designing flows.
It was about designing trust in voice —
in conditions that were designed to make trust difficult.
04 / The Design Logic

Flow Architecture
as Trust Architecture.

Every flow was built around one question before anything else: what is the worst thing that can happen here, and how do we prevent it?

Not as a compliance checklist. As the actual starting point for design.

Clear entry and exit points — no dead ends, no loops.

A customer who can’t find their way out of a voice flow doesn’t hang up neutral. They hang up angry, or frightened, or both. Every flow had a designed exit at every stage — not just at completion.

Mandatory confirmation before any irreversible action.

Before any transaction executes, any card is blocked, any security action is taken — Dottie reads back exactly what is about to happen, in plain language, and waits for explicit confirmation.

“You want to pay ₹5,000 to your ICICI credit card ending 4321, correct?”

This isn’t a UX nicety. It’s the moment the system proves it understood — and gives the customer one final chance to catch a misunderstanding before it becomes a problem.

The confirmation prompt is the trust architecture made audible.

Layered fallbacks — gentle first, always.

If the system doesn’t understand, it doesn’t fail loudly. It re-prompts, once, in different words. If the second attempt fails, it rephrases and tries once more. If it still can’t resolve, it hands to a human agent — with context preserved, so the customer doesn’t have to start over.

The layered fallback isn’t a safety net. It is the primary design. A voicebot that handles everything perfectly 95% of the time and fails abruptly the other 5% is not a trustworthy voicebot. The quality of the failure is as designed as the quality of the success.

Microcopy as Precision Instrument

When you’re asking a bot to lock your card, words are not decoration.

The difference between acceptable and excellent copy in financial voice UX isn’t tone. It’s certainty transfer — does the user know, after hearing the response, exactly what state their account is in?

Bad copy: “Card blocked successfully.”

The user knows the action completed. They don’t know what it means.

Better copy: “Your card is now blocked. No further transactions can go through.”

Same information. But now the user knows: what happened, and what the consequence is. Two sentences. Complete certainty.

This distinction was applied across every response in every flow. Not as a style preference — as a functional requirement. In a medium where the user cannot see a screen, cannot re-read a message, cannot check a status indicator — the words are the interface.

The tone defined for the system: professional, empathetic, precise.

Not warm-and-casual. Not cold-and-corporate. The register of a very competent bank employee who genuinely wants to help and knows exactly what they’re doing.

↓ Too casual → feels careless with serious things.
↓ Too technical → feels like the system is protecting itself, not the user.
✓ The balance → authority without distance. Precision without coldness.

Collaboration Beyond Design

This project blurred the lines between design, ML, and compliance in ways that couldn’t be separated.

01

Intent architecture with ML engineers.

“Block my card”, “stop my card”, “my card’s gone”, “cancel my card” — four different utterances, one intent. The intent hierarchy had to be designed carefully: broad enough to catch natural variation, precise enough not to trigger the wrong flow. This was design work, ML work, and annotation work simultaneously.

02

API mapping with solution architects.

Backend systems don’t speak human. Every API response — account status, transaction state, confirmation codes — had to be translated into language a person could hear once, understand immediately, and act on. This translation layer was a design responsibility, not an engineering one.

03

Annotation guidance with data teams.

The quality of the ML model depended directly on the quality of the training data. Defining annotation rules — how to tag Indian English date expressions, how to handle code-switching, how to capture the difference between “I think my card might be missing” and “Block my card immediately” — was part of the design process.

04

Voice alignment with the bank.

The bot’s personality, phrasing, and escalation language had to be approved through ICICI’s brand and compliance layers. Getting that alignment required translating design decisions into arguments that compliance teams could evaluate — and learning, in both directions, about what the constraints actually were.

The real design skill on this project wasn’t conversation design.
It was working across four different professional languages simultaneously — and making decisions that held up in all of them.
05 / What the 70 Flows Taught

Scale Reveals
What Single-Flow
Design Hides.

When you design one flow, you can treat edge cases as exceptions. When you design seventy, edge cases become the curriculum.

Every unusual utterance, every interrupted sentence, every moment where a customer’s real need didn’t match the flow’s expected path — each one was a design problem that had to be resolved before it became a live failure.

Three things that only become visible at scale:

Intent collision.

At 70 flows, the same word can mean something different in different contexts. “Cancel” means one thing mid-booking and something else when said to a card management flow. The system had to be designed to resolve these collisions — using conversational context, not just keywords.

Confidence calibration.

How certain does the NLP need to be before it acts? Too high a threshold and the system asks for clarification constantly, feeling broken. Too low and it acts on misheard input, which in a financial context is dangerous. Every flow had a different confidence threshold, calibrated to its specific risk profile.

The asymmetry of failure.

A failed payment confirmation is a recoverable error. A failed card unblock when a customer is trying to use their card abroad at midnight is a different kind of failure. The 70 flows weren’t equally weighted — the design had to reflect that some failures matter much more than others.

06 / The Outcome

Proving NLP-Powered
Banking on Call
Was Trustworthy.

✓ 70+ live use cases, from everyday tasks — checking balances, recent transactions — to critical security actions — card blocking, PIN resets, fraud reporting.
✓ A measurable reduction in contact center dependency for repetitive but urgent requests. The flows that previously required a human agent — because IVR menus couldn’t handle natural language — now resolve end-to-end in voice.
✓ One of the first transactional voicebots in India in a regulated financial environment, proving that NLP-powered banking on call was not only possible but trustworthy enough for customers to use for actions that matter.

08 / Reflection

Every Failure
as a Design Surface.

The most important design decision on this project wasn’t a conversation flow.

It was the decision to treat every failure as a design surface — not a gap in the system, but a moment that needed as much attention as the happy path.

Financial voice UX is unforgiving not because customers are demanding, but because the stakes are real. A wrong confirmation, an unclear response, an abrupt escalation — in any other context, these are minor frustrations. Here, they are the difference between a customer who trusts the system and one who never calls again.

The confirmation prompt — “Your card is now blocked. No further transactions can go through” — is a small thing. Twenty words.

But those twenty words are doing something specific: they are transferring the system’s certainty to the user. Completing the loop. Making sure that the action the user intended is the action the user believes just happened.

That is what trust in voice looks like at the level of a sentence.

And building seventy flows’ worth of those sentences — in a language the NLP could parse, the compliance team could approve, and a nervous customer could hear and immediately understand — was the whole project.

Designing for money means designing for the moment when someone needs to be absolutely certain that the system heard exactly what they said.

Every word is load-bearing.
Every confirmation is a promise.
Every fallback is a relationship saved.

Every decision about when to act
and when to stop is baked into
the architecture — before a single

Trust in Voice.
CS 06 · Service Design · Spatial UX · Wayfinding · 2023

The
Invisible
Guide.

You don’t need to create curiosity. You just need to stop punishing it.
WILD
01 / The Person

The Fork
in the Road.

Picture a parent on a Sunday morning.

They’ve brought the family to Van Vihar — a 4.5 km protected forest in Bhopal. Rescued tigers, leopards, sloth bears. The kind of place that should feel like an adventure.

They’re standing at a fork in the road.

To the left: an unmarked path disappearing into trees. No sign. No indication of what’s down there or whether it loops back.

To the right: the main road. Straight, legible — and somewhere down it, the lion enclosure the kids have been asking about since the car.

They go right.

Not because they lack curiosity. Because they carry a responsibility this environment keeps ignoring.

A wrong turn means a tired child, a frustrated partner, twenty minutes gone with nothing to show for it. The rational choice is to stay on the road that goes somewhere known.

This is not a design problem about curiosity.
It’s a design problem about cost.

02 / The Park

Grown Organically.
Never Designed.

Van Vihar had grown organically over decades. Attractions accumulated. The map expanded. The visitor experience hadn’t kept pace.

The website: tariffs, timings, rules. No sense of what waited inside.

The entry: cash-only queues. Even “online” booking required offline verification. The day began with bureaucracy before a single animal had been seen.

The road: one long main path acting as a default. Forks appeared without signal.

Visitors followed the road because nothing else communicated anything.

Discovery collapsed into a checklist.

“Hum to Lion Tiger Crocodile pe hi rukte hai.”

“We only stop at the lion, tiger, crocodile.” — said not with disappointment, but as fact.

Somewhere past one of those unmarked forks: a butterfly garden. 2,500 butterflies, 50+ species — one of the most quietly extraordinary places in central India.

80% of first-time visitors left without finding it.

03 / The Research

Going in
as Strangers.

The research started with immersion.

We traced the park as strangers — trying to understand how Van Vihar reveals itself to someone who doesn’t already know it.

Then shadowing real visitors — families, couples, groups — from entry to exit.

The pattern was consistent: the main road captured almost everyone. Side trails appeared on almost no one’s path.

Blueprinting the System

The blueprint made the structural problem legible.

The system had been optimised for transactions — timings, tickets, rules. Not for experiences — discovery, story, delight.

Every touchpoint was functional. None was designed to build curiosity or make a fork feel like anything other than a risk.

Two People, One Problem

The research produced two archetypes — not demographics, but two different relationships with uncertainty in an unfamiliar place.

The Responsible Caretaker
Safety &
Predictability
The parent at the fork. The spouse who organised the outing. The person whose measure of a good day is whether everyone had one.
  • Motivated by: safety, predictability, collective wellbeing
  • Main road, visible attractions, nothing that requires a gamble
  • Not timidity — responsibility. A wrong turn has a social cost
The diagnostic figureDesigning for their constraint solves the problem for everyone.
The Experience Seeker
Discovery &
Novelty
The couple looking for something unexpected. The young friends who came for the unusual.
  • Motivated by: novelty, hidden places, the feeling of discovery
  • Explores when curiosity is sparked — skips anything bureaucratic
  • Not a lack of curiosity. The absence of a cue.
What stops themSomething just legible enough to make the next step feel like discovery rather than a gamble.

The insight beneath both:

Neither persona was failing to be curious.

Both were making rational decisions given what the environment told them: this fork is unresolved. Proceed at your own risk.

The problem was never curiosity.
The problem was that the park kept punishing it.
04 / The Three Tensions

Three Traps
the Obvious Solutions
Fell Into.

The research produced three interlocked tensions — each one a trap the obvious solutions fell into.

01
Clarity × Curiosity

Too much clarity — signs, arrows, routing — and the visit becomes a guided tour. Too little, and the fork stays a gamble. Guide just enough to let wonder unfold.

02
Nature × Service

Van Vihar’s value is its naturalness. Any service layer — technology, signage, systems — risks becoming louder than what it supports. Service must enable nature, not compete with it.

03
Visible × Invisible

Guidance too visible kills the sense of wandering. Guidance too invisible leaves discovery to chance — and as the 80% figure proved, chance is not a design strategy. Cues felt more than seen.

Why the Obvious Solutions Failed

Every rejected intervention failed at one of the three tensions.

× Park-wide signage — Breaks Clarity × Curiosity. Every sign removes a discovery.
× Painted arrows — Breaks Visible × Invisible. Guidance becomes prescription.
× Treasure hunt app — Breaks Nature × Service. The game becomes louder than the forest.
× Audio guide — Breaks Nature × Service. You listen to the park instead of being in it.

Saying no was as important as saying yes.

Each rejection sharpened what the Invisible Guide had to be: subtle enough to preserve wonder, present enough to eliminate the gamble.

05 / The Invisible Guide

What Does a Fork
Feel Like at Zero Cost?

What does a fork in the road feel like when the cost of taking it is zero?

Not: how do we tell visitors where to go. But: how do we make the wrong turn stop existing as a concept?

Three moments in the visit. Three nearly invisible interventions.
Together — a completely different relationship with the park.

At the Gate — Frustration → Curiosity

The visit currently begins with cash, a form, a queue, and offline verification of an online booking.

The redesign: a single QR at the entry. Scan. Everything opens.

More important than eliminating friction: it shifts the register of arrival. The first thing the visit asks isn’t prove you have a ticket — it’s what might you find in here?

The Caretaker’s anxiety drops the moment they feel oriented. The Seeker’s curiosity activates the moment entry stops feeling like an obstacle.

Both people enter differently. And a visit that begins differently, continues differently.

On the Road — Gamble → Invitation

This is the intervention that removes the wrong turn as a concept.

At key junctions: zebra stripes on the path. Bold enough to notice. Natural enough not to feel like signage.

You look down — a stripe. You look at the map — the same stripe, same junction. The fork resolves instantly. The environment and the tool speak the same language.

For the Responsible Caretaker
The gamble
disappears.
The fork is legible.
Turning is no longer
a risk — it’s just
a direction.
For the Experience Seeker
The stripe
is a cue.
Something worth
following.
Curiosity now has
a thread to pull.

The wrong turn stops existing. Every fork becomes an invitation.

At the Enclosure — Species → Story

The existing panels: dense text, species names, conservation statistics. Designed for experts. Read by almost nobody.

The redesign: a tap on the map reveals a short visual story. The rescue. The recovery. The individual.

The park stops being a checklist of species and becomes a place of specific encounters.

The Caretaker has something to tell their child. The Seeker has the depth they came for. The visit, heading toward lion, tiger, crocodile, exit, starts extending — because there are more stories, and the map keeps offering them.

The System

By the end of the walk, you’ve barely noticed the guide.

  • The QR reduced the administrative overhead of entry
  • The stripes made every fork legible
  • The map’s zoom levels revealed the park the way curiosity works — more the deeper you go
  • The stories turned enclosures into encounters

None of it shouted. None of it dictated.

The Caretaker moved through the park with less anxiety than they’ve ever felt in an unfamiliar place — without once feeling managed.

The Seeker followed threads they felt like they’d chosen themselves — without once feeling directed.

Both people moved.
Neither felt guided.
That is the Invisible Guide.

06 / Proof

The Campus
Pilot.

Before bringing the Invisible Guide to Van Vihar, the concept needed a controlled test.

We piloted it on a university campus. Hidden courtyards, quiet gardens, rarely-visited nooks became the “attractions.”

80% of students scanned the QR immediately — the hesitation at entry, gone.

Students discovered places they’d never visited despite months of attendance. They described the experience as “a treasure hunt.”

No treasure hunt had been designed. The system had simply stopped punishing curiosity.

08 / Reflection

The Park Wasn’t
Broken.

The visitors weren’t incurious.

The system was asking visitors to resolve forks the environment had no business leaving unresolved — to gamble their afternoon for a butterfly garden they didn’t know existed.

Designing for the Responsible Caretaker wasn’t a constraint. It was the diagnostic lens that made everything else clear.

Remove the cost of the wrong turn for the person carrying the most responsibility, and you remove it for everyone. The Seeker benefits from the same cue, for different reasons. Both people move.

The Invisible Guide works not because it helps curious people find things.

It works because it removes what was making responsible people behave as if they weren’t curious — when they were, the whole time, waiting for somewhere safe to spend it.

You don’t need to create curiosity.
You just need to stop punishing it.

Somewhere past that unmarked fork, past the junction with the stripe — the butterfly garden was always there.

We just made it findable.

The bikes sat still. Visible everywhere. Used by almost no one.

A product visible to all, used by none — is worse than invisible.

Business Model → Neglected UX

Revenue came from advertising on docking stations — not from rides. So rides were never optimized. The customer experience was a cost center, not a revenue driver.

Result: UX investment was structurally disincentivized

Result: The service couldn’t explain itself

Bikes everywhere but rarely used = seemed like a failed service. Users assumed if it were good, others would be using it. Low usage validated low usage.

Presence without participation reads as abandonment.
